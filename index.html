How ColorScoPe Works

Step 1

An urban area is identified to need infrastructural support.

Step 2

The approved aerial image of the area is uploaded to ColorScope.

Step 3

CSP’s unique image processing algorithm measures the road widths.

Step 4

A user controlled custom filter highlights roads of certain widths.

Step 5

Traffic chokepoints, overly narrowed bus lanes, etc can be identified and rectified.

Our Mission

Roughly one million cars alone enter São Paulo daily. Creating and maintaining effective transportation infrastructure is a major issue relevant to cities across the world. However, there is no single path to improving mobility, even throughout areas of the same city. ColorScoPe allows for a flexible, in-depth look at road infrastructure of specific areas, thus providing tools for innovative and effective urban management to address city-users' needs and maintain a vision of integration, connnectedness, and sustainability in the modern world.

The App

ColorScoPe documents the existing precedents of road infrastructure, and simplifies an aerial image to highlight such. Our mission is to support up-to-date spatial planning for provision of urban services and safety in São Paulo, especially at the local metropolitan level.

Implications

ColorScoPe provides a tool to filter the map by street width, to decide how narrow is "too narrow" and how wide is "too wide" for any specific area. Widening roads can resolve bottlenecks, for example. However, narrow roads--which have been linked to decreased vehicle speeds--could prove beneficial for residential areas.

Why road width?

In a UNHABITAT study of 30 cities, notable characteristics included the percent of land allocated for streets, as well as number of street intersections for each area. Well-planned cities show higher levels in both categories, supporting that the width of roads is a vital aspect of urban planning. (Source: "The Relevance of Street Patterns and Public Space in Urban Areas." UN-Habitat. UN-Habitat, 2013. Web. 13 Jan. 2016.)

Current Projects

The Mario Covas Rodoanel Beltway, a São Paulo metropolitan ring road, provides a way for vehicles to circumnavigate the inner city area and thus reduces traffic congestion. (Source: www.newgeography.com)

Future Steps

ColorScoPe can be updated to show newly constructed roads, by uploading an aerial image for the background. Additionally, acknowledging which vehicles utilize certain roads most frequently can further specify appropriate actions to improve mobility. Future features could also include detection of paved and unpaved streets, broadening the area of analysis, and presence of sidewalks, good lighting and more.
